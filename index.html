Mathematical Framework

The theoretical foundations behind the optimization model.

🗺️

Problem Understanding

A waste collection vehicle must visit 6 zones exactly once and return to the depot (Zone 1). Each arc between zones carries a distance and a delay cost. The challenge is finding the tour with minimum total cost.

📐

Assumptions

  • Single vehicle, single depot at Zone 1
  • All zones must be visited exactly once
  • Distance & delay values are deterministic
  • Directed graph with 6 nodes and 30 arcs
  • λ is a non-negative scalar known a priori
📊

Mathematical Model

Let dij be the distance and tij be the delay from zone i to zone j. The edge cost is defined as:

cij = dij + λ · tij

where λ ≥ 0 is a weighting coefficient controlling the trade-off between travel distance and time delay.

⚙️

Optimization Formulation

Minimize the total tour cost:

min Σi≠j cij · xij

Subject to: each zone entered & exited exactly once, and subtour elimination constraints (Miller–Tucker–Zemlin). Binary decision variables xij ∈ {0, 1}.

🧠

Algorithm Design

With only 6 zones, brute-force enumeration is feasible: (6−1)! = 120 permutations. Each permutation is evaluated by summing edge costs along the tour. The permutation with the minimum sum is selected as the optimal route.

The route 1→2→4→5→6→3→1 achieves the globally minimum cost of 49 for λ=1, visiting all collection zones efficiently before returning to the depot.

💡

Insights

  • Higher λ favors low-delay routes over short-distance ones
  • Zone 2 and 4 form a natural cluster — low inter-cost
  • Zone 3 is best visited last before depot return
  • Zones 5 & 6 share a low-delay corridor
✅

Conclusion

Brute-force TSP is viable for small networks (n ≤ 10). For larger city-scale deployments, heuristics like Nearest Neighbor, 2-opt, or metaheuristics (GA, ACO) should be employed. The λ-weighted cost function provides a flexible framework adaptable to real-world logistics constraints.
